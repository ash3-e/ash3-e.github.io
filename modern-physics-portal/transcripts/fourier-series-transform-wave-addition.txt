Fourier Series - Transform -Wave Addition
Overview
This page is about decomposing waves into modes and then recombining those modes to make localized packets and interference patterns. The note links classical Fourier analysis to quantum superposition and to the passage between real space and $k$-space.

Fourier language
On a finite interval one expands a function in sines and cosines:
$$f(x)=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\left[a_n\cos\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)+b_n\sin\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)\right].$$
The coefficients are obtained by projection,
$$a_n=\frac{1}{L}\int_{-L}^L f(x)\cos\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)dx, \qquad
b_n=\frac{1}{L}\int_{-L}^L f(x)\sin\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)dx.$$
In the continuum limit the same idea becomes the Fourier transform.

Wave packets
The page is using superposition to explain how a packet is assembled from many nearby wave numbers. A narrow spread in $k$ gives a broad feature in $x$, while a broad spread in $k$ gives a more localized packet. That is the natural bridge to matter-wave packets and uncertainty-style reasoning.

Wave addition
The sheet also appears to use addition identities to discuss constructive and destructive interference, beats, envelopes, and the difference between carrier oscillation and packet motion. In quantum mechanics that same algebra becomes amplitude addition rather than direct intensity addition.
