Bohemian Pilot Waves — plaintext transcript

Overview
These notes summarize the Bohmian or pilot-wave interpretation of nonrelativistic quantum mechanics. The wavefunction is written in polar form so that the Schrödinger equation separates into a continuity equation for probability density and a Hamilton–Jacobi-like equation with an extra quantum potential term. In this picture, particles always have positions, and the wave guides the particle trajectory.

Core rewrite
Start from the polar decomposition
$$\psi = R e^{iS/\hbar}.$$
The probability density is
$$\rho = R^2 = |\psi|^2.$$
Substituting this form into the Schrödinger equation gives a continuity equation
$$\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} + \nabla \cdot \left(\rho \frac{\nabla S}{m}\right)=0,$$
so probability flows like a conserved fluid. The guidance velocity is
$$\mathbf{v} = \frac{\nabla S}{m},$$
which means the particle momentum is
$$\mathbf{p} = \nabla S.$$

The second real equation looks like a modified classical Hamilton–Jacobi equation,
$$\frac{\partial S}{\partial t} + \frac{(\nabla S)^2}{2m} + U + Q = 0,$$
but it contains the quantum potential
$$Q = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{\nabla^2 R}{R}.$$
This extra term is what produces nonclassical trajectories and interference even though the particle always has a definite position.

Interpretation notes
Measurement probabilities still obey the Born rule
$$P(\text{measurement}) = |\psi|^2,$$
but Bohmian mechanics interprets collapse differently: the wave evolves by the Schrödinger equation while effective collapse appears because branches of the wavefunction no longer guide the actual particle configuration. The notes also mention nodes, strange-looking trajectories, noncrossing behavior near nodes, and the fact that standard Bohmian mechanics is most natural for nonrelativistic particles with mass. Relativistic extensions and photons are harder to treat cleanly.
