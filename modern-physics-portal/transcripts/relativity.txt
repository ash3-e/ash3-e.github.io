Relativity — plaintext transcript

Overview
This compact sheet introduces the transition from Newtonian or Galilean kinematics to special relativity. The note derives time dilation from a light-clock argument, discusses simultaneity failure, introduces the rear-clock-ahead effect, and rewrites velocity addition for moving reference frames.

Core rewrite
Under Galilean motion one would write
$$\Delta x=\Delta x' + v\Delta t', \qquad \Delta t=\Delta t',$$
but this fails when the speed of light must be the same in every inertial frame. A light-clock round-trip argument gives the relativistic factor
$$\gamma=\frac{1}{\sqrt{1-v^2/c^2}},$$
and the dilated time interval
$$\Delta t=\gamma t_0.$$

Light-clock relation
The page writes the round-trip light speed schematically as
$$c=\frac{2L}{t_A+t_B},$$
then shows how moving clocks appear slower by the factor $$\gamma$$.

Simultaneity and velocity addition
Relativity of simultaneity appears as a clock offset, summarized here by the rear-clock-ahead expression
$$\Delta t_{RL}=\frac{Lv}{c^2}.$$
Motion between frames is not simple arithmetic; instead the note records the relativistic addition law
$$u'=\frac{v+u}{1+vu/c^2}.$$
The sheet also uses geometric velocity decompositions such as $$u=\sqrt{u_p^2+u_s^2}$$ to discuss component reasoning in diagrams.
