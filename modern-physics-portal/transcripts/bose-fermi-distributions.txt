Bose-fermi Distributions
Overview
These notes compare bosonic and fermionic occupation statistics in thermal equilibrium. The page moves among counting arguments, detailed balance, density of states, and condensed-matter examples such as superconductivity, semiconductors, and Cooper pairing.

Equilibrium distributions
The central occupation factors are
$$f_B(E)=\frac{1}{e^{(E-\mu)/k_B T}-1}, \qquad
f_F(E)=\frac{1}{e^{(E-\mu)/k_B T}+1}.$$
The number of occupied states in an interval is built from the density of states:
$$N(E)\,dE = D(E) f(E)\, dE.$$
The bosonic minus sign allows unrestricted occupation of one state, while the fermionic plus sign encodes Pauli blocking.

Main idea of the page
The sheet is organizing three layers at once. First is state counting: how many modes are available near a given energy. Second is thermal occupation: how likely those modes are to be filled at temperature $T$ and chemical potential $\mu$. Third is materials interpretation: why the same mathematics leads to very different behavior for photons and phonons, electrons in a metal, and carriers in a semiconductor.

Condensed-matter remarks
For bosons, low-energy occupation can become large and can support collective phenomena. For fermions, occupancy is limited to zero or one per single-particle state, so low-temperature behavior is controlled by the Fermi surface and Fermi energy. The page also seems to note that Cooper pairs behave effectively like bosons even though they are built from fermions.
