Diffusion And Drift — plaintext transcript

Overview
This sheet rewrites the transport notes on random walks, diffusion, carrier drift in electric fields, conductivity, mobility, scattering time, mean free path, and the Einstein relation linking diffusion and mobility.

Core rewrite
Random motion spreads particles in time. For one-dimensional diffusion the mean-square displacement is
$$\langle x^2 \rangle = 2Dt,$$
where $$D$$ is the diffusion constant. The diffusive particle current points down the density gradient:
$$J_{\text{diff}} = -D \nabla n.$$
Combining conservation of particles with this law gives the diffusion equation
$$\frac{\partial n}{\partial t} = D \nabla^2 n.$$

Drift section
In an applied electric field carriers acquire an average drift velocity
$$v_d = \mu E,$$
where $$\mu$$ is the mobility. The drift current is
$$J_{\text{drift}} = q n \mu E = q n v_d.$$
This leads to conductivity
$$\sigma = q n \mu.$$

Microscopic picture
The notes connect transport to scattering through a mean scattering time $$\tau$$ and mean free path
$$\ell = v \tau.$$
A key bridge between thermal diffusion and electrical response is the Einstein relation
$$D = \mu \frac{k_B T}{q}.$$
The overall message is that microscopic random motion plus scattering produces macroscopic conductivity, diffusion, and current flow.
