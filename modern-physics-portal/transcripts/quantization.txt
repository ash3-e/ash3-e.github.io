Quantization
Overview
These notes collect several examples of quantization: a particle in a box, the harmonic oscillator, finite potentials, and cavity photon modes. The page is using boundary conditions to explain why only certain wavelengths, energies, or excitation numbers are allowed.

Bound-state quantization
For a one-dimensional box the allowed stationary states are
$$\psi_n(x)=\sqrt{\frac{2}{L}}\sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right), \qquad
k_n = \frac{n\pi}{L},$$
which leads to
$$E_n = \frac{\hbar^2 k_n^2}{2m} = \frac{n^2 \pi^2 \hbar^2}{2mL^2}.$$
The physical reason is the boundary condition: only standing waves that fit the geometry survive.

Broader message
The page widens the same idea to other systems. In a harmonic potential the normalizable solutions have discrete energies. In an optical cavity only certain wavelengths fit the cavity. In second-quantized language one then also quantizes how many excitations occupy a given allowed mode.

Two kinds of quantization
So the sheet is really about two layers at once: quantized mode structure from boundaries and quantized excitation number once the allowed modes are known.
